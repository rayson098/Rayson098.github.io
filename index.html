Chapter 01

What is a seizure?

A seizure is not a diagnosis by itself. It is a transient event caused by abnormal neuronal activity.

“A seizure is a transient occurrence of signs or symptoms caused by abnormal excessive or synchronous neuronal activity in the brain.”

Normal neuron

  • Excitation–inhibition balance
  • Glutamate-mediated excitation
  • GABA-mediated inhibition
  • Depolarization balanced by hyperpolarization

Seizure state

  • Excessive excitation
  • Hypersynchronous firing
  • ↑ Na⁺ channel activity
  • ↓ K⁺ channel activity and ↓ GABA

Seizure ≠ Epilepsy

Seizure is a single clinical event. Epilepsy is a chronic predisposition to recurrent unprovoked seizures. A single provoked seizure does not constitute epilepsy.

Chapter 02

How does a seizure spread?

The key transition is from local depolarization to network recruitment.

Seizure cascade

Normal activityExcitation and inhibition remain balanced.
InitiationExcessive neuronal depolarization begins.
HypersynchronyNeurons fire together abnormally.
PropagationNeighboring neurons are recruited.

Paroxysmal depolarization shift

Depolarization phase: Ca²⁺ influx opens voltage-gated Na⁺ channels, producing repetitive action potentials.

Hyperpolarization phase: GABA receptors and K⁺ channels normally terminate the burst. Failure of this afterhyperpolarization allows sustained seizure activity.

Propagation mechanisms

  • ↑ extracellular K⁺ lowers neuronal firing threshold.
  • Presynaptic Ca²⁺ accumulation increases neurotransmitter release.
  • NMDA activation enhances glutamate-mediated excitation.
  • Ephaptic interactions alter electrical coupling between neurons.
Chapter 03

Classification begins with onset.

The ILAE framework organizes seizures by onset, awareness, motor/non-motor features, and observable manifestations.

ILAE 2017

  • Focal onset
  • Generalized onset
  • Unknown onset
  • Unclassified

ILAE 2025 update

  • Awareness and responsiveness
  • With or without observable manifestation
  • Chronological semiology
  • Improved clinical localization

Clinical logic

Start with what was observed. If onset is unwitnessed, avoid forcing focal or generalized onset. Combine semiology with EEG, imaging, CSF, metabolic workup, and clinical context.

Quiz 1

A young woman awakens to find her 20-year-old boyfriend having a seizure in bed. The onset is unwitnessed. She describes bilateral stiffening followed by bilateral rhythmic shaking. EEG and MRI are normal.
Answer: Bilateral tonic–clonic seizure, unknown whether focal or generalized.
Because the onset was not witnessed, there is insufficient evidence to determine focal vs generalized onset. The observed manifestation is bilateral tonic–clonic activity.

Quiz 2

A 25-year-old woman has seizures beginning with an intense sensation that “familiar music is playing.” She hears people talking but cannot understand them. Witnesses report that she does not respond to verbal or tactile stimuli. Afterward, she is mildly confused.
Answer: Focal impaired consciousness seizure.
The chronological evolution is auditory aura → receptive aphasia → impaired responsiveness → postictal confusion.

Quiz 3

A 22-year-old man remains fully aware during seizures. He reports that the hair on his arms “stands on edge” and experiences a flushing sensation.
Answer: Focal preserved consciousness seizure.
Awareness is preserved. The observable manifestations are autonomic: piloerection and flushing.

Quiz 4

A 14-month-old girl has brief clusters of sudden bilateral arm extension and trunk flexion lasting about 2 seconds. EEG demonstrates hypsarrhythmia with bilateral spikes, predominantly over the left parietal region. MRI reveals left parietal cortical dysplasia.
Answer: Focal seizure with epileptic spasms.
The focal evidence is left parietal cortical dysplasia on MRI and focal predominance on EEG.

Quiz 5

A 33-year-old man with prior febrile seizures develops recurrent episodes beginning with abdominal discomfort followed by impaired awareness. Witnesses describe lip smacking, fumbling hand movements, and occasional right-hand dystonic posturing.
Answer: Focal impaired consciousness seizure.
The evolution is epigastric aura → impaired awareness → oroalimentary automatisms → gestural automatisms → dystonic posturing.
Chapter 04

Focal motor seizure.

A focal cortical network produces prominent motor manifestations.

Definition

Focal seizures arise from a neuronal network localized within one brain region or more broadly distributed within one cerebral hemisphere.

Key bedside rule: motor manifestations are usually contralateral to the seizure focus.

Motor manifestations

  • Tonic movements
  • Clonic movements
  • Myoclonic movements
  • Facial twitching
  • Eye deviation

Special phenomena

  • Jacksonian march
  • Todd’s paralysis
  • Epilepsia partialis continua

Etiologies of Focal Motor Seizure

The causes of focal motor seizure can be organized by the ILAE etiologic framework. In this case, the most clinically relevant category is infection, especially HSV encephalitis involving the temporal lobe.

Genetic
  • Inherited or de novo mutations
  • SCN1A mutation: Dravet syndrome
  • KCNQ2 / KCNQ3 potassium channel gene mutations
Structural
  • Acquired: stroke, trauma, brain tumor
  • Hypoxic brain injury
  • Degenerative brain disease
  • Genetic: malformations of cortical development
Metabolic
  • Mitochondrial disease
  • Aminoacidopathies
  • Urea cycle disorder
Infectious
  • Acute symptomatic seizure → infectious etiology epilepsy
  • HSV encephalitis
  • HIV, tuberculosis, parasitic infection
Immune
  • Anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis
  • Anti-LGI1 encephalitis
  • Other autoimmune encephalitides
Unknown
  • No definite cause identified after evaluation

Key point: Infection is the most common etiology worldwide.

Chapter 06

EEG localization.

EEG is supportive evidence for cortical hyperexcitability and localization.

The 10–20 system

F = frontal, T = temporal, C = central, P = parietal, O = occipital. Odd numbers denote the left side; even numbers denote the right side.

Focal epileptiform discharges

  • Localized to specific electrode regions
  • Reflect focal cortical irritability
  • Correlate with contralateral motor signs
  • Help localize the seizure onset zone
